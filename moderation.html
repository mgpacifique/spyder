In our previous analysis, we determined that crater diameter explains approximately 23.6% of the variance in crater depth. To understand the "lurking variables" that account for the remaining 76.4%, we will introduce a moderator: the morphology of the crater's ejecta.

Testing a Potential Moderator

A moderator is a third variable that affects the strength or direction of the relationship between two primary variables. By splitting our data into subgroups based on the morphology_group categorical variable, we can calculate the Pearson Correlation Coefficient for each subgroup and see if the association changes significantly depending on the ejecta type.

marscrater.ipynb
Python 
import scipy.stats

# Calculate correlation moderated by Morphology Group
groups = clean_data['morphology_group'].unique()

print("Correlation between Diameter and Depth moderated by Morphology Group:\n")

for group in groups:
    sub_data = clean_data[clean_data['morphology_group'] == group]
    if len(sub_data) > 2: 
        corr, p = scipy.stats.pearsonr(sub_data['DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE'], sub_data['DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG'])
        r_sq = corr**2
        print(f"Group: {group} (n={len(sub_data)})")
        print(f"r = {corr:.4f}, r^2 = {r_sq:.4f}")
        print("-" * 30)

Geological Interpretation


To determine if the relationship between crater diameter and depth is consistent across different types of impacts, a moderator analysis was conducted using the categorical variable morphology_group (ejecta pattern). For the entire sample, crater diameter explains approximately 23.6% (r² = 0.2362) of the variance in crater depth.


However, when examining the subgroups, we found that morphology significantly moderates this association. The correlation is exceptionally strong for craters with "Multi Layer" (r = 0.79, r² = 0.63) and "Double Layer" (r = 0.77, r² = 0.60) ejecta patterns, meaning diameter directly explains over 60% of the variance in depth for these impacts. In contrast, for "Single Layer" craters, the relationship is much weaker (r = 0.56, r² = 0.31).


This indicates that environmental factors and geological complexity heavily dictate how a crater's width impacts its ultimate depth. The "lurking variable" accounting for much of the unexplained variance in our original correlation was indeed the morphological type of the impact.

A Note on Post Hoc Testing for Peer Review


Unlike an ANOVA or Chi-Square test, which produce a single "omnibus" p-value that requires post hoc testing (like Tukey's HSD or Bonferroni adjustments) to determine which specific groups differ, testing a categorical moderator with a Pearson Correlation is handled differently. By stratifying the data by the morphology_group moderator and calculating the correlation coefficient separately for each subgroup, the specific relationships have already been isolated. Therefore, no additional post hoc testing is required for this analysis.
