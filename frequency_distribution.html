Before you can say anything meaningful about data, you have to look at it — really look at it. This post is about that first look: running frequency distributions on three variables from the Mars crater database and figuring out what kind of data we're actually working with.

The dataset is the Robbins Global Mars Crater Database, a catalog of every confirmed impact crater on the Martian surface down to 1 km in diameter. It contains 384,343 rows. My three variables are crater diameter, crater depth, and ejecta morphology classification — chosen because they sit at the heart of the research question I developed in Week 1: do larger craters become proportionally shallower over time?

Dataset: Robbins, S. J. (2019). Mars Global Crater Catalog. NASA Planetary Data System.

Variables selected: DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE  ·  DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG  ·  MORPHOLOGY_EJECTA_1

Course: Data Analysis and Interpretation — Week 2, Frequency Distributions

The Program

The code is straightforward. I'm using pandas.cut() to bin the two continuous variables into ten equal-width intervals, then value_counts() to count each bin. For the categorical variable, value_counts(dropna=False) is essential — I need NaN to appear in the output rather than get silently discarded.

marscrater.ipynb
Python 
## Importing modules: pandas and numpy alongside the mars crater dataset
import pandas as pd
import numpy  as np

data = pd.read_csv("marscrater_pds.csv", low_memory=False)

## ── Variable 1: DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE (diameter, numeric) ──
diam_freq  = pd.cut(data['DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE'], bins=10)
diam_table = diam_freq.value_counts().sort_index().reset_index()
diam_table.columns = ['Diameter Range (km)', 'Count']
diam_table['Percentage (%)'] = (
    diam_table['Count'] / diam_table['Count'].sum() * 100
).round(2)
print(diam_table)

## ── Variable 2: DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG (depth, numeric) ──
depth_freq  = pd.cut(data['DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG'], bins=10)
depth_table = depth_freq.value_counts().sort_index().reset_index()
depth_table.columns = ['Depth Range (km)', 'Count']
print(depth_table)

## ── Variable 3: MORPHOLOGY_EJECTA_1 (ejecta type, categorical) ──
morph_table = data['MORPHOLOGY_EJECTA_1'].value_counts(dropna=False).reset_index()
morph_table.columns = ['Ejecta Morphology', 'Count']
print(morph_table)

Diameter — complete data, extreme skew

The diameter distribution is about as lopsided as a distribution can get. 384,160 craters — 99.95% of the entire catalog — fall within the first bin, meaning their diameter is below roughly 117 km. The remaining bins are nearly empty: 148 craters between 117 and 234 km, 24 between 234 and 350 km, and at the far end, two outliers exceeding 1,000 km. Those two are almost certainly recording Hellas Planitia (~2,300 km) and Argyre (~1,200 km), the two largest confirmed impact basins on Mars.

There is no missing data here at all. Every crater has a diameter because diameter is the primary attribute used to identify and catalog craters in the first place — you can't have a crater without measuring it. The shape of the distribution is a classic power law: the same type seen in asteroid size populations, where small bodies vastly outnumber large ones. This means the diameter variable is both complete and reliable, but its extreme skewness means any regression or correlation analysis will need a log-transformation before parametric statistics apply.

For the next step

A log-transformed diameter variable will almost certainly be necessary before any regression against depth. The raw distribution violates every assumption of normality.

Depth — structural missing data encoded as zero

The depth table looks, at first, like another right-skewed distribution. But there's something more significant going on. That dominant first bin — 325,454 craters with a recorded depth between −0.425 and 0.117 km — contains a large number of craters with depth exactly equal to zero. Not craters that are flat. Craters that were never depth-measured.

This is a structural data problem, and it's the most important finding from the whole analysis. When depth = 0, it almost certainly means the topographic data didn't exist or wasn't processed for that crater, not that the crater has no bowl. Among the craters that do carry a genuine positive depth measurement, the distribution tapers rapidly: 45,769 craters between 0.12 and 0.65 km, another 10,194 between 0.65 and 1.19 km, and only 8 craters deeper than 3.3 km. The maximum recorded depth is ~4.95 km.

Missing data encoded as zero

Before any diameter–depth analysis, the data must be filtered to DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG > 0. Treating zero-depth craters as measured observations would collapse the relationship we're trying to study. This filter reduces the working dataset to approximately 76,800 craters — still a very rich sample.

Ejecta morphology — a sparse, long-tailed categorical

The third variable is the most visually dramatic to look at and the hardest to work with. Of the 384,343 craters in the database, the majority have a blank entry for ejecta morphology. Among those that do have a label, there are 156 unique strings — but most appear so rarely that they're effectively noise in any aggregate analysis. The most common non-null label, "Rd" (interpreted as radial ejecta), stands well above the rest, followed by a handful of more specific morphology codes that each account for a fraction of a percent of all craters.

The sparsity isn't random. Ejecta blankets erode. On a planet where wind, dust, and ancient volcanic resurfacing have been active for billions of years, the physical structure that would let a geologist classify the ejecta type simply doesn't survive for most craters. What we have is a useful variable for studying the subset of craters where preservation was good — but it cannot be analyzed across the full dataset without severe selection bias.

Summary

All three variables are non-normally distributed. Diameter is complete but requires log-transformation. Depth carries structural missing data that must be filtered before any analysis. Ejecta morphology is too sparse for full-dataset use and should only be studied on a preserved-crater subset. These constraints will directly shape every analytical decision in the weeks ahead.
