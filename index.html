Scars on Mars:
What Crater Depth Tells Us About a Planet's Ancient History

Pacifique Manzi Robbins Global Mars Crater Database
Pacifique Manzi
Posted for Data Analysis and Interpretation — Wesleyan University via Coursera  ·  Week 1 Assignment

Investigating the association between Impact Crater Diameter and Depth Using the Robbins Global Martian Crater Database.

When I first opened the Mars Crater codebook, I honestly wasn't sure what I was looking at. Over three hundred thousand impact craters, each one catalogued with coordinates, size, depth, morphology, ejecta type, and a degradation score. It felt overwhelming in the best possible way.

Mars is one of the most cratered surfaces in the solar system that we can actually study up close, and unlike Earth (where erosion, tectonics, and life erase impact craters within millions of years), Mars preserves craters across billions of years of history. Every crater is, in a sense, a frozen moment, a snapshot of what the planet looked like the day something slammed into it. That's what pulled me in.

The Research Question

The simplest, most elegant question I could ask with this dataset is:

Are bigger craters deeper?

It sounds almost too obvious to be interesting. Of course a bigger impact would make a deeper hole, right? But here's where it gets genuinely surprising: the answer is yes, but not in the way you'd expect. And why it's not proportional turns out to be one of the most fascinating stories in planetary science.

My Variables

From the Robbins codebook, I selected two primary variables:

DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE

Crater diameter in kilometers, measured from the best-fit circle to the crater rim in orbital images.
Role: Independent variable (X)

DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG

Crater depth in kilometers, measured from the average rim height to the average floor elevation using MOLA topographic data.
Role: Dependent variable (Y)

I also included several contextual variables for my personal codebook:

LATITUDE_CIRCLE_IMAGE

Crater center latitude (°N).
Why I kept it: Latitude reflects geological age and terrain type — key control variable.

LONGITUDE_CIRCLE_IMAGE

Crater center longitude (°E).
Why I kept it: For spatial mapping.

MORPHOLOGY_EJECTA_1

Ejecta layer morphology classification.
Why I kept it: Signals subsurface ice content at time of impact.

NUMBER_LAYERS

Number of ejecta layers.
Why I kept it: Proxy for target material composition.

DEGRADATION_STATE

Crater preservation quality (1–5 scale).
Why I kept it: Controls for erosion in analysis.

A note on filtering: 307,529 craters in the dataset (roughly 80%) have a recorded depth of 0. This means depth was either unmeasurable or not recorded typically for very small or very degraded craters. For my analysis I will filter to craters where DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG > 0, leaving approximately 76,800 craters. This is still an extraordinarily rich sample.

The Second Topic: Geological Modification Over Time

Here's is where the research question gets layered. My independent variable, diameter, isn't just a measure of how big the impactor was. It's also a proxy for how much has happened to the crater since it formed. Bigger craters have been sitting on the Martian surface longer on average, exposed to wind erosion, dust deposition, volcanic resurfacing, and ancient water activity. They've had more time to be filled in.

So my second topic is post-impact crater infilling and preservation, and I'm asking whether the depth deficit we see in larger craters is the result of physics (large craters are inherently flatter even when fresh) or geology (large craters have been filled in over billions of years), or, most likely, both.

What the Literature Says

Before forming my hypothesis, I reviewed five key resources. Here's what they collectively taught me:

1. The Physics of Why Large Craters Are Shallow
Wünnemann & Ivanov (2003) — "Numerical modelling of the impact crater depth–diameter dependence in an acoustically fluidized target"

This paper was the first thing that changed my intuition. Using 2D computer simulations of the entire impact process, Wünnemann and Ivanov showed that crater depth is not a simple linear function of diameter; it's governed by a phenomenon called acoustic fluidization. Here's the mechanism: when a larger impactor strikes, the shockwave temporarily causes the surrounding rock to behave like a fluid. The crater floor bounces upward, a process called central peak uplift, and the walls collapse inward, terrace by terrace. What you're left with is not a deep bowl but a wide, flat-floored structure with a central mountain and stepped walls. Planetary scientists call these complex craters, and they are by definition shallower relative to their diameter than their smaller, bowl-shaped cousins (simple craters).

On Mars specifically, the transition from simple to complex craters occurs at diameters between 5 and 8 km, smaller than on the Moon because Mars has higher surface gravity. What this means for my analysis: even before erosion touches a crater, the depth-diameter relationship is already sub-linear. Doubling the diameter never doubles the depth.

2. The Best Empirical Benchmark
Tornabene et al. (2017) — "A depth versus diameter scaling relationship for the best-preserved melt-bearing complex craters on Mars"

If Wünnemann & Ivanov gave me the theory, Tornabene and colleagues gave me the number to test against. Their team identified 224 craters on Mars containing "pitted floor materials", distinctive deposits of impact melt that form only right after the crater is created and that signal minimal subsequent modification. These are the freshest craters on Mars.

After removing any crater distorted by pre-existing topography, they measured 92 craters carefully and derived a best-fit scaling law: depth = 0.323 * diameter^0.538. That exponent, 0.538, is the critical number. Because it is less than 1, depth grows slower than diameter. A crater ten times wider is not ten times deeper; it's only about 3.4 times deeper. And this is for the freshest craters on Mars, before any infilling occurs.

When I run my own regression on the full Robbins dataset (which includes heavily eroded craters), I expect my exponent to be even lower than 0.538. How much lower will tell me how much geological modification has occurred across the planetary surface as a whole.

3. The Historical Map of Mars
Carr (1980) — "The Morphology of the Martian Surface"

Michael Carr's sweeping 1980 survey is the foundational map of Mars's geology. Writing before most modern spacecraft data existed, Carr documented every major terrain type. Two of his observations are directly relevant to my project:

  • The north-south crustal dichotomy: The southern hemisphere sits 1-3 km above the topographic datum. The northern hemisphere is lower, younger, and far more sparsely cratered; it was resurfaced by volcanism after most of the large craters had already formed. This means latitude in my dataset is a genuine geological variable.
  • Ground ice and layered ejecta: Carr observed that Martian craters are surrounded by layered ejecta blankets with terminal ridges, a morphology completely unlike lunar craters. His explanation: large amounts of water ice in the subsurface. When an impactor struck, it didn't just excavate rock, it melted ice and produced a fluid-like ejecta flow, which affects crater depth.
4. A Crater's Life After Impact
Le Deit et al. (2013) — "Sequence of infilling events in Gale Crater, Mars"

This paper is the most visually compelling, telling the story of one specific crater—Gale Crater, a 150 km wide basin now occupied by NASA's Curiosity rover. Using orbital imagery and topographic data, Le Deit and colleagues reconstructed the complete infilling sequence of Gale over approximately 3.6 billion years.

The great central mound (Mount Sharp) is built primarily from airfall material, dust, volcanic ash, deposited year after year and cemented shut by sulfate minerals. A shallow lake formed in the deepest portions later. A periglacial environment characterized most of Gale's history, producing rock glaciers. Wind erosion has been the dominant recent force.

For my project, Gale Crater is the most powerful argument that large craters lose their depth through multiple distinct mechanisms acting over billions of years. Every process described reduces the value of DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG. When my regression shows that large craters are proportionally shallower, Le Deit et al. tells me at least some of that signal is real geological history, not an artifact of measurement.

5. The Database Itself
Robbins (2011) — "Planetary Surface Properties, Cratering Physics, and the Volcanic History of Mars from a New Global Martian Crater Database"

No source is more important to cite than the one that created the data I am analyzing. Robbins's dissertation describes not just the methodology of building the database but also his own depth-diameter analysis. Key findings include:

  • The simple-to-complex transition on Mars occurs between 5 and 8 km diameter.
  • The deepest craters at any given diameter are the best proxies for fresh, unmodified morphology. The average relationship across all craters is significantly shallower because the average includes heavily degraded examples.
  • Layered ejecta craters cluster at low-to-mid latitudes, consistent with ice-rich target material.
My Hypothesis

Based on the convergence of these five sources, I am ready to state a formal hypothesis:

Crater diameter (DIAM_CIRCLE_IMAGE) will be positively but sub-linearly associated with crater depth (DEPTH_RIMFLOOR_TOPOG) in the Robbins Mars crater database, such that larger craters are proportionally shallower than smaller craters. The depth-diameter scaling exponent derived from the full dataset will be lower than the 0.538 benchmark established by Tornabene et al. (2017) for best-preserved craters, reflecting the cumulative effect of billions of years of aeolian infilling, fluvial sedimentation, and volcanic resurfacing that preferentially reduces the measurable depth of larger, older craters.

In plain English: yes, bigger craters are deeper — but not nearly as much deeper as you'd expect. Physics makes them flat from birth, and time has been quietly filling them in ever since.

Why This Matters

Mars's craters are not just geological curiosities. They are time capsules. The depth-diameter relationship, measured across the entire planetary surface, is a fingerprint of billions of years of climate and geological history, ancient lakes, glaciers, volcanic eruptions, dust storms. Every deviation from the expected scaling law is a clue that something happened in that spot, at some point, that we don't yet fully understand.

That's what I'm going to spend the rest of this course trying to read.
